Ph.D. Dissertation · Carnegie Mellon University

Depicting Places in Information Systems

Closing the Gap Between Representation and Experience

The apps we open every day have quietly become the lens through which we see the places around us. Yet that software still treats a place as a dot on a map — a coordinate, a star rating, a boundary on a screen — flattening the centers of meaning, memory, and shared experience where life actually happens. My dissertation is about the gap between how our information systems represent places and how we actually live them — and why closing it matters for people and places alike.

What's at stake

Places are more than their locations

We find restaurants on Yelp, get directions from Google Maps, plan trips on Airbnb, and form impressions of our cities from Instagram. Over the last three decades, these systems have quietly become the middleware between people and the physical world — increasingly shaping how we encounter, choose, and remember the places in our lives.

But they are remarkably reductive about it: a place becomes a point in 2D space, an averaged star rating, a fixed boundary on a map. That flattening doesn't stay on the screen. These representations feed consequential decisions — where capital flows, which neighborhoods get investment, what researchers conclude — and when they are impoverished, the cost lands on real communities, in perpetuated stereotypes and erased cultural identity.

“What begins as undifferentiated space becomes place as we get to know it better and endow it with value.” Yi-Fu Tuan, Space and Place, 1977

My thesis: by closing the gaps between how places are represented and how they are experienced, we can design social information systems that are more insightful and more meaningful — not just for the people who use them, but for everyone who lives in the places they depict.

The gaps

Four truths about place that software misses

Places are dynamic

Systems treat a place as fixed context. Real places are ecosystems in constant motion — reshaped by social, political, cultural, and natural forces, and different from season to season, even from day to night.

Places are multitudinous

A place gets one identity and one rating. But every place contains multitudes — as many identities as the people and communities who claim it, each with their own perspective.

Places can be placeless

Systems demand a fixed location. Yet some places move (the bar car of a commuter train), and some have no coordinates at all (an online community).

Places are blended

Software treats the physical and the digital as separate worlds. In practice, the boundary between a place and its digital reflection is increasingly — and productively — blurry.

The work

Three systems, built and studied in the wild

Rather than argue from theory, I took a research-through-design approach: I designed, built, and evaluated three working systems, each closing a different gap. Together they trace a path toward location-based software that feels less like a database and more like a place.

Livehoods · ICWSM 2012 · Best Paper Award · Test of Time Award (2022)

Livehoods

How can a map of a city evolve the way the city itself does?

Municipal maps draw neighborhoods as fixed areas that tile the city and rarely change. Livehoods instead discovers neighborhoods from the ground up, clustering more than 18 million location check-ins shared on social media to reveal the collective activity of a city — a map that can evolve as neighborhoods do. An interview study with 27 Pittsburgh residents probed how these data-driven boundaries relate to both lived experience and the official municipal borders, surfacing stories of transition, identity, and the digital divide.

Curated City · CHI 2014

Curated City

How can a city guide hold more than one truth?

Ratings collapse a place into a single number; reviews bury it in long, subjective prose. Curated City is a social city guide that represents places as a multitude of personal experiences instead of one averaged verdict. People create and remix each other's guides, so the individual and collective views of the city coexist — accentuating the commonalities across experiences without forcing anyone to label a place simply “good” or “bad.” A two-week field trial with 20 Pittsburgh residents produced nine design implications for guides built on this plurality.

Journeys & Notes · CHI 2016

Journeys & Notes

How do you give a sense of place to somewhere that isn't one?

Airports, overpasses, rest stops, bus terminals — the anthropologist Marc Augé called these non-places: transient, undifferentiated spaces that lack the qualities for a sense of place to take root, yet fill an ever-larger share of modern life. Journeys & Notes is an Android app that turns the time we spend in motion into a shared virtual place, where travelers connect with one another and reflect on the moment. A large-scale field study reached 9,435 participants, showing how social computing can nurture place where the physical world offers none.

The framework

Six dimensions of place, for the people who build with it

I distilled what I learned building these systems — together with the place literature in geography, anthropology, and sociology — into six qualitative dimensions of place, paired with 18 guiding questions, to help designers reason about place as a lived space rather than a coordinate.

Scale

Places range from the small and personal to the vast and all-encompassing.

Transformation

Places are always in a state of change — across years and across a single day.

Interconnectedness

Tangled webs of links between people, objects, infrastructure, and other places.

Openness

Flows of people, culture, ideas, and capital pass into and out of a place, across borders.

Throwntogetherness

The chance of distinct lives and trajectories meeting — or not — at the same place.

Politics

Places are governed by power, and that power is never evenly apportioned.

Why it matters now

The stakes only grow from here

As information systems become ever more the lens through which we encounter the world, the cost of getting place wrong only compounds. The Livehoods paper won Best Paper at ICWSM in 2012, and ten years later the same venue awarded it the Test of Time Award, which recognizes research whose influence has endured.

And the lens is shifting again. People increasingly ask large language models what a neighborhood is like, where they should go, and whether to move there — delegating not just how they find places, but how they reason and decide about them. These models inherit the same flattened representations, now spoken back in a single, confident voice. Whose version of a place gets encoded — and what gets lost — only grows more consequential from here.

The north star of this work is a future where the sense of a place flows seamlessly between the physical world and its virtual counterparts, and where location-based systems are more place-like. This dissertation is a set of concrete steps toward that vision — in working systems, and in ideas other builders can take further.
